Introduction:

All in all, I wrote around a dozen academic papers throughout my college career. However, I've compiled just four of them to display here. I chose some of them because the topics intrigued me and I had a lot of fun writing them, others I chose because I labored long hours to write them and I am proud of the result. Each is different, written to fulfill unique set of criteria, and formatted accordingly.

Faith, Ethics, and Absurdity:

A Study of Kierkegaard and “Faith by Virtue of the Absurd”

One of the most fascinating philosophers in the 1800’s was Soren Kierkegaard. His philosophy differed greatly from the mainstream philosophy of his time. He is known by many as the father of existentialism. In this paper I will detail many of Kierkegaard’s ideas by explaining what a “teleological suspension of the ethical” and “faith by virtue of the absurd” mean, along with discussing how the story of Abraham and Isaac supports Kierkegaard’s ideas, and what my opinion is concerning the matter.

Kierkegaard famously said, “truth is subjectivity.” This sounds alarming at first, because if truth is merely subjective, then anyone can get away with doing what they “believe” because it’s true. Except that’s not what Kierkegaard means when he says “truth is subjectivity.” He means that finding objective truth still involves a choosing, that an attitude towards a belief is just as important as the belief itself. It’s how one follows an objective truth that matters.

This philosophy was novel in its time. However, now we can read about many philosophies detailing similar ideas. This sect of philosophy is called Existentialism. Since Kierkagaard’s ideas were some of the first he is often considered to be “The Father of Existentialism.” Existentialism has come to mean “any philosophy that asserts the most fundamental questions involve meaning and choice.” These themes differ greatly from the majority of philosophies, and deals with the individual’s quest for truth rather than truth itself.

Kierkegaard describes three distinctly different lives a person can lead. The detailing of these three lives certainly formulate an existentialist argument, of which Kierkegaard has become famous for. The three types of lives are the aesthetic life, the ethical life, and the religious life.

The aesthetic life is one of personal gratification. It is often described as an “immersion in sensuous experience; valorization of possibility over actuality; egotism; fragmentation of subject of experience; nihilistic wielding of irony and skepticism; and flight from boredom.” Sounds great doesn’t it? Kierkegaard doesn’t think so. Instead he says this type of life is inauthentic and not free. In a way the aesthetic life lets you lead a life of luxury, but after a while you don’t govern your own choices. Instead, addictions and worldly illusions dictated your actions.

The second life is called the ethical life. It differs from the aesthetic life in many ways except for one. It’s still inauthentic and not free. In an ethical life a person will conform to a certain moral code. In many ways they will adopt “the universal” or “prevailing social norms” of the time. Merely adopting a moral code doesn’t liberate someone because they are still a part of “the crowd.” They will not make choices that are vital to their salvation based on the fact that they are restrained by societies code.

The religious life, however, is the only authentic and free life. It’s the best life, yet it is full of anxiety, despair, angst, and forlornness. Not many people make it to a religious life. The religious life demands a “terrible responsibility” of individuals, which causes much anxiety, “for upon h/er existential choices hangs h/er eternal salvation or damnation.” Another key point Kierkegaard points out about the religious life is that, “If we choose faith we must suspend our reason in order to believe in something higher than reason. In fact we must believe by virtue of the absurd.”

Virtue of the absurd is a notion that God will demand certain things from his children that defy logic. Only after one lets go of logic and has faith will they be enabled to live a religious life. Job, in the Bible, “gets everything back again by virtue of the absurd,” and Kierkegaard himself “hoped to get Regine back again after breaking off their engagement, by virtue of the absurd.” Like “faith by virtue of the absurd,” a “teleological suspension of the ethical” will require people to forsake logic by demanding moral people to do seemingly immoral things in the name of faith.

In the Bible, the story of Abraham and Isaac illustrates both “faith by virtue of the absurd” and a “teleological suspension of the ethical.” In the book of Genesis, Chapter 22, it states, “God did tempt Abraham, and said unto him, Abraham... Take now thy son, thine only son Isaac, whom thou lovest, and... offer him there for a burnt offering upon one of the mountains which I will tell thee of.” It’s interesting to note that the Hebrew word for “tempt” is “test” or “prove.” In many ways God was testing Abraham. God asks Abraham to do the absurd and to sacrifice his own son. To any moral person this would be considered murder, which is frowned upon greatly.

So what does Abraham do? If he was living an aesthetic life, he would ignore God, return and rebuild Sodom and Gomorrah, and dwell there for the rest of his life. If he was living an ethical life, he would scoff at the idea of “murder,” and stick to his society’s moral code. If he was living a religious life, he would follow God by virtue of the absurd, and do what God commanded, even if it seemed like God had commanded something seemingly unethical. Abraham demonstrates his faith to God by taking Isaac up to the mountain to sacrifice him.

Kierkegaard stated that “the story of Abraham contains a teleological suspension of the ethical.” Meaning the God who said, “Thou shalt not kill.” is the same God who commanded Abraham to sacrifice his own son. It would seem like God operates on a higher law than his previously revealed set of ethics. If Abraham were to follow God’s command to kill Isaac, would he be acting immorally? This brings into question God’s ethics. Kierkegaard says in regard to God’s law and worldly ethics, “For the genuinely religious person there is a higher and quite different source and form of obligation.” Abraham trusts religiously in God, ignoring ethics as he knew them, and listens to God by sacrificing his son.

In the Bible there doesn’t seem to be much anxiety on the part of Abraham. But if we are to impose natural human emotion into the story, one can imagine a distraught Abraham going to sacrifice his son. His only son. He is definitely living the religious life, which causes him despair and anxiety and forlornness, like Kierkegaard mentioned anyone to live the religious life would experience. However, Abraham is rewarded greatly for his faith. He doesn’t have to kill his son after all, and is promised that “all the nations of the earth” will be blessed because of his obedience.

Ultimately, by using this story, Kierkegaard wants to illustrate that to have true faith in God, a person must give up logic. Was it logical for Abraham to sacrifice his son? Certainly not. Did Abraham have faith in God? Without a doubt. Abraham was free from the restrictions of both the aesthetic and ethical lifestyle, and dedicated to a higher law through faith. Kierkegaard’s argument in providing this story is that faith will always require you to do illogical things, even if they aren’t as drastic as in the case of Abraham. Kierkegaard says that Christianity “embodies paradoxes which are offensive to reason.” He uses the example of an eternal God becoming the finite form of Jesus. The only way to truly embrace this belief is by virtue of the absurd.

I, personally, agree with Kierkegaard. I have beliefs that I’d rather not try and prove logically, but that I’ve felt are true. These sort of beliefs must be approached with the right attitude or they will not do me any good. Any person who adopts such beliefs would have to be religious about them, or they suddenly become just a set of restrictions that govern their lives. On the other hand, if a person is to abandon their set of ethics all together, they don’t have much choice than to lead the aesthetic lifestyle, which isn’t any better than the ethical. So whatever the belief, a person must be religious about it in the quest for truth. It’s the only way to live a free life.

However, “Faith by virtue of the absurd” does present some major issues, because throughout history people have done wild things in the name of God, and Kierkegaard seems to be justifying them to some degree. His argument could give way for radicals of all religions, but I don’t think this is what he intends to happen. Kierkegaard’s example of Abraham and Isaac is just an extreme example of “faith by virtue of the absurd” and a “teleological suspension of the ethical.” Ultimately, his argument encourages people to live religiously, and to own their choices and lives. Kierkegaard promotes a life of passion and fulfillment in the lives of the individual, which I think is worth the risk of a few radicals. Amen, I say unto Kierkegaard’s argument, amen.

To Kill a Mockingbird

Should It Still Be Studied In School?

Harper Lee’s book, To Kill a Mockingbird, is often hailed as the greatest book of this century. It was released on July 11, 1960, where it found a spot atop the bestseller list. The book comfortably remained on the list for 80 weeks following its release, and is celebrated today as, “one of the 10 bestselling novels of all time.” (Who Was Atticus Finch?). To Kill a Mockingbird has had a rich history in the world of literature, which is quite a contrast to the ugly, American history that it portrays in its text. Because statistics attest that the book has captivated thousands of readers, the question we ask ourselves now isn’t whether To Kill a Mockingbird is a good story, rather is it a valuable text? By examining the book through the lens of a historical critic, and exploring the sociological events that Harper Lee experienced in her life, I aim to prove that To Kill a Mockingbird, is indeed valuable, despite its narrator’s narrow lens and critical claims that the book is pure “sentiment.” To Kill a Mockingbird, not only portrays a concentrated portion of a larger history, but also manages to teach lessons learned throughout history, making it a valuable text worth studying in school.

Texts of all kinds can be critiqued using an array of different critical theories. In a chapter, which details historical criticism, we learn, “Formalist criticism in its purest form, for example, assumes that everything relevant to a discussion of a particular literary text may be found in or deduced from that text. Historical criticism assumes the opposite: the text itself is insufficient to explain the text.” Therefore, a historical critic must look outside the text to find its true value. They must look at history. To Kill a Mockingbird (herein referred to as Mockingbird), as a literary text, details and discusses many historical issues that were prevalent in the early 1930’s, such as racism, gender discrimination, and the struggles of social class. However, in order to truly gauge if Mockingbird is valuable, one must not only examine the events that occur in the book, but ultimately compare them with events that actually occurred. In other words, are the events that happen in the book true to the nature of history? A reader wouldn’t be surprised to discover that Harper Lee herself witnessed events similar to those that were depicted in her book, which adds value to the book’s narration, making it not only a historical text, but a fictional representation of history itself.

Maycomb county, for example, is almost identical to Monroeville, Alabama, the city where Harper Lee was born and raised. The honorable lawyer, Atticus Finch, was greatly inspired by Harper’s own father, Amasa Lee. Even the book’s narrator, Jean Louise “Scout” Finch, is considered to be a representation of Harper Lee. Whether the reader is aware of it or not, Mockingbird is as valuable as the history it represents.

However, some critics fail to consider the historical events that Harper Lee experienced during her life and hastily conclude that Mockingbird isn’t valuable. In a critique of the book Harold Bloom says, “The crisis of (Scout’s) book confirm her in her intrinsic strength and goodness, without wounding her sensibility or modifying her view of reality.” It would seem like Harold Bloom wanted to see Scout struggle, deeply and personally, in Mockingbird. Instead, Scout views societies conflict from a far and enjoys her childhood, occasionally learning a lesson or two along the way. In his article, Just How Good Is ‘To Kill a Mockingbird’?, William Giraldi mentions that unlike other books with heavy racial themes, Mockingbird is unusually “sentimental.” He essentially agrees with Harold. Their question: how can a book, which clearly wishes to instill change in its audience, possibly demand change from its readers when the book’s main characters don’t experience a drastic change themselves?

Perhaps, the change doesn’t need to be drastic in order to be significant. History might provide the answer. What sort of challenges did the young Harper Lee face? In Who Was Atticus Finch?, an article written by Talmage Boston, he states, “Like Scout, the author enjoyed the company of a peculiar summertime friend, and was intrigued by a young man on her street mysteriously imprisoned in his own home . . . Writing a book 20 years after her childhood, she described what she had seen, felt, and remembered in her . . . hometown.” In other words, readers are experiencing history as Harper experienced it, whether it be what they see or feel while they are reading. She, like Scout, was curious, and enjoyed running around Monroe in the summer with the boys. The ultimate reality is Harper Lee may not have been personally affected by society’s faults in her childhood, and if she wasn’t, then why should Scout?

A similar truth is embedded in the pages of Mockingbird. Some people were happy in the early 1900’s, despite racial slurs and other forms of social discrimination. That’s the truth. Kids were innocent. Did the end of the book depict a crippled Scout, haunted and torn by a harsh society? No. She was still a kid. She was still making sense of everything. The book is “sentimental” because we experience the story through the eyes of a child. In a way, this is the perfect way to tell a story such as this, because a child doesn’t understand injustice. After learning about Hitler in school Scout asks Jem about Miss Gates hypocrisy, “How can you hate Hitler so bad an’ then turn around and be ugly about folks right at home?” Seeing a girl trying to come to grips with living in an imperfect society, and still remain happy, is beautiful. It’s beautiful, and it happened, which means we can do it too.

Even if we were to entertain Harold’s critique of Mockingbird, and assume Scout didn’t struggle personally, it wouldn’t hold true. Scout does struggle, just not in the way critics might want her to. At school Scout gets taunted by Cecil Jacobs because Atticus is defending Tom Robinson, a local African American. She knows she shouldn’t fight him, but wants to defend her father, so she fights him anyway. She recalls, “I was far too old and too big for such childish things, and the sooner I learned to hold in, the better off everyone would be. I soon forgot. Cecil Jacobs made me forget.” Considering how young Scout is, she handles her emotions fairly well. However, it’s easy to make mistakes when we are young. The next day Scout returns to school, and for the first time in her life steps down from a fight. That’s personal growth! Scout not beating the little twerp a second time is only one example from a whole school of lessons she learned throughout the book, which implies Scout is fluid in her beliefs, and absorbs truth easily. Sadly, there comes a time in almost everybody’s life when their beliefs become concrete, even when they have much to learn. That’s when innocence dies. That’s the difference between the common white male in Mockingbird, and Scout.

The same can be said about Harper’s life, or anybody’s life for that matter. We do not wake up one day to some drastic experience that changes our lives forever, rather we experience little changes each day. The sum of those little changes can prove to be most valuable, especially if we remain innocent and constantly allow our beliefs to change. The fact that Harper Lee wrote a book based on her life is evidence that she experienced a gradual change. Two lessons we can learn from Harper’s history is that change occurs slowly, and when we become concrete in our beliefs we hurt not only our personal well-being, but society’s welfare too. Those lessons are presented to us throughout both history and Mockingbird, which makes it a valuable text.

Referring back to the article, Just How Good Is ‘To Kill a Mockingbird’?, William Giraldi further illustrates his point when he compares Harper Lee’s book to Mark Twain’s, Huckleberry Finn. He states, “Huckleberry Finn isn’t feel-good because it demands action of us: It petitions us for sacrifice, and the typical American would rather not consider that.” He continues by saying the opposite of Mockingbird: it doesn’t demand sacrifice of us, which is why Americans cling to it. By saying that the book doesn’t challenge our moral character, William inherently implies the book doesn’t hold any real value. William mockingly states, “As long as there are Atticuses in the world, all will be well, and you yourself can remain recumbent.”

If our cynical friend, William, were to step away from fiction and examine the reality behind Mockingbird, he might change his mind about it. Does Mockingbird petition us to sacrifcice? Surely, it does. Only not in the same way Huckleberry Finn does. The reason so many people cherish Mockingbird is because it challenges readers to change in a personal way. In Huckleberry Finn, Huck risks his life to save Jim, a slave that has escaped. Despite such a noble cause, most people aren’t going to lay down their lives, especially when it comes to changing political norms, because politics don’t change overnight, even with a martyrdom. If anyone knows how to handle politics, it would be Atticus Finch. After all, he is a lawyer. After Scout gets in a fight at school Atticus teaches her a valuable principle. He says, “You just hold your head high and keep those fists down. No matter what anybody says to you, don’t you let ‘em get your goat. Try fighting with your head for a change.” His philosophy is not to fight harder, but to fight smarter. Atticus knows that the best way to change law would be to set an example, encourage others to be better, and combat the roots of racism where it counts, which would be to challenge society’s moral compass in a reasonable manner. Atticus’s strategy in combatting discrimination may feel strange at times, but it makes sense if you consider the historical figure that inspired Atticus’s character.

Under the mask of Atticus Finch lies Amasa Lee, Harper’s own father. Amasa led a similar life to his fictional counterpart, Atticus Finch. Both of them were single parents, were lawyers in Alabama, and courageously stared down racial threats.

Amasa Lee, as editor and publisher of the Monroe Journal, followed the case of Walter Lett, an African-American convicted of rape. In many ways Walter’s case resembled that of Tom Robinson’s in Mockingbird. Like Tom Robinson, Walter was also sentenced to death. The Alabama Board of Pardons later changed his sentence to life in prison after a campaign “led by Amasa Lee” wrote letters stating they “felt the evidence had not established the defendant’s guilt.” This is a great example of a simple act that yielded small, but significant results. The campaign surely sparked a worthwhile conversation in Monroe, from which they were able to mold a better society.

Reminiscent of Amasa’s campaign is Atticus’s experience in the courthouse defending Tom. Like Amasa Lee, Atticus Finch knew his audience. He knew winning the case wasn’t about defending Tom, but about challenging the white juries prejudice. His final words in the court were, “Gentlemen, a court is no better than each man of you sitting before me on this jury. A court is only as sound as its jury, and a jury is only as sound as the men who make it up. I am confident that you gentlemen will review without passion the evidence you have heard, come to a decision, and restore this defendant to his family. In the name of God, do your duty.” His words weren’t defending Tom. They were convicting the jury. He was fighting smarter. Despite losing the trial, a little progress was made. In the words of Miss Maudie Maycomb County was, with the help of Atticus, making a “baby-step” toward a better society.

If there is one thing that Harper’s history, as experienced outside the text of Mockingbird, can offer, it is that the small things in life matter. Mockingbird might not petition readers to act drastically, like critics may demand of other books, but it petitions readers in other valuable ways. It makes the reader feel as if happiness can be found in perilous times, if one is to remain innocent. Scout, a historical representation of Harper Lee, demonstrates innocence by constantly learning throughout Mockingbird. History teaches us that we can be tolerant of other’s beliefs, while still instigating a moral change in their character, but one must learn to live comfortably with themselves first. This is reinforced by Amasa, who wrote letters petitioning Walter’s false conviction, and Atticus, who lived honorably, and challenged the jury in defense of Tom Robinson. Each yielding small, but significant results. History supports the value of these lessons, and they have proven worthwhile in the life of those that have been influenced by either Harper and Amasa Lee, or Scout and Atticus Finch.

Mockingbird is based heavily on the life of Harper Lee, who experienced history in a very unique way. It is valuable to readers from a historical perspective because it not only teaches a portion of valuable history, but also allows history to reinforce valuable lessons taught in Mockingbird, which challenge readers to grow personally.

When Superheroes Fall:

The Effects of Disconfirming Communication, the Exit Response, and Lack of Self-Disclosure in Captain America: Civil War

What would you get if Stan Lee, the man behind Marvel Comics, and an interpersonal scholar decided to make a movie? You would get something like Captain America: Civil War (hereafter referred to as Civil War), which combines aspects of realistic interpersonal communication with those not-so-realistic aspects typical of the super hero genre: intense sequences of fighting involving the use unnatural superpowers set in a climate of heightened global conflict. Once a viewer can see past the action and spectacle of Civil War, it is about friends trying to work through differences. Essentially, the movie is about the main characters, Steve Rogers and Tony Stark, trying to work through their differences, but both feel strongly that they are right. In this essay I intend to, first, provide a synopsis of the Civil War, then demonstrate how the use of disconfirming communication (evaluation and certainty), instead of confirming communication (provisionalism), and a lack of self-disclosure between characters, leads to exit frequent exit responses that further perpetuates conflict. Finally, I will offer advice directed mostly at Roger, which will help him quickly resolve an issue, without so much fighting, should he find themselves in a similar circumstance in the future.

Civil War follows the events of Captain America: Winter Soldier (hereafter referred to as Winter Soldier) and Avengers: Age of Ultron (hereafter referred to as Age of Ultron). In order to understand why Steve Rogers (AKA Captain America) and Tony Stark (AKA Iron Man) act the way they do in Civil War, one must understand what they experienced in their past. In Winter Soldier, Rogers finds himself caught up in a web of lies and conspiracy, when he learns that SHIELD, an organization he once trusted, has secretly been infiltrated by Hydra, an evil and powerful organization that seeks world domination. Rogers, who eventually takes down Hydra and foils their attempt at world domination, is now hesitant to trust organizations that may have hidden agendas, including his very own government. A few months later, Tony Stark proposes an idea, Project Ultron, that may assist the Avengers, a group of superheroes dedicated to saving humanity, if they need it in the future. Tony’s idea is to use technology and the power of an infinity stone, which contains intelligence inside it, to create an advanced robotic task force that can help ease the burden of the Avengers. Even after Rogers, a fellow member of the Avengers, advised Stark not to mess with the power of the infinity stone, Stark proceeds with his project. Shortly after Ultron, the highly intelligent robot, is created, it learns of the terrible things humans have done and decides to destroy humanity entirely. Stark, Rogers, and other superheroes eventually put an end to Ultron, but not before many innocent civilians were killed in the destruction caused by their intense battle with Ultron.

Because of this destruction and death caused in Age of Ultron, the United Nations is pressured by Earth’s citizens to control the Avengers in Civil War. The government proposes the Sokovia Accords, a superhero registration system through which the government would dictate what the Avengers can and cannot do, almost as if they were weapons of mass destruction. Feeling the need to be put in check after causing so much devastation with Ultron, Stark is an advocate for the Sokovia Accords. Rogers, however, refuses to sign them and give up his freedom, knowing full well that there may be a sinister motive behind the accords. Their differences regarding this issue drives the conflict throughout the movie.

Neither Stark nor Rogers hesitate to assert, with certainty, that their point of view is one hundred percent correct, which is a from disconfirming language discussed in (Wood, 2014). Referred to as certainty, it is the opposite of provisionalism, or the willingness to compromise. “We communicate certainty by using language that is absolute and often dogmatic. This kind of language suggests that there is one and only on answer, valid point of view, or reasonable course of action” (Wood, 2014, p. 231). To illustrate their use of disconfirming language, I will use quotes from a few scenes in the movie.

After the Sokovia Accords are proposed, the Avengers meet to discuss their plan of action, but Stark doesn’t want to hear the opinions of others. “There’s no decision-making process here,” he states. “We need to be put in check, whatever form that take, I’m game. If we can’t accept limitation, then we are no better than the bad guys.” After Stark communicates his ideas with such certainty, Rogers can react in one of two ways. He can either defend his own opinions or he can stay quiet. Of course, Rogers responds to the conflict with certainty of his own, voicing his opinion. “Tony, if we sign this, we surrender our right to choose. What if this panel sends us somewhere we don’t think we should go? What if there’s somewhere we need to go and they don’t let us? We may not be perfect, but the safest hands are still our own,” he says. Both heroes believe with certainty that they are doing the right thing, and by doing so, disqualify the opinion of the other. This is a clear example of certainty as a form of disconfirming communication.

Despite their use of disconfirming language, I argue that during the first part of the movie they are attempting to maintain their relationship by working towards a solution, which (Wood, 2014) refers to as the voice response to conflict. The voice response to conflict is the healthiest and most constructive of the four responses to conflict discussed in (Wood, 2014). The voice response is described as a means to directly address and resolve conflict. “Voice implies that people care enough about a relationship to notice when something is wrong and do something to improve the situation” (Wood, 2014, p. 261-62) Rogers and Stark are using their voice to try and figure out a solution, but the original conversation is cut short when Rogers receives a text that a dear friend has passed away. Though his accuse for exiting is valid, Rogers still responds by physically leaving the conflict. Later, Stark and Rogers try again to work actively toward a solution, responding to conflict with voice once more. However, after using more disconfirming communication, the only way for Rogers to respond to the conflict is to exit.

“Sometimes, I want to punch you in your perfect teeth, but I don’t want to see you gone,” Stark says as he tries to convince Rogers to sign the Sokovia Accords for the second time. “We need you Cap. So far nothing has happened that can’t be undone. Just sign.” In this situation, which takes place a day or so after Rogers initially walks away from the conflict, it is interesting to note that a small incident of self-disclosure on Stark’s part is immediately followed by confirming communication from Rogers. (Wood, 2014) describes self-disclosure as “the intentional revelation of personal information about ourselves that others are unlikely to discover in other ways” (p. 222). Studies have shown that “as a relationship develops further, the breadth and depth of self-disclosure grows. When a relationship deteriorates, levels of self-disclosure usually decrease again” (Utz, 2015). Not only does Stark disclose why he wants Rogers to sign, but also discloses why he, personally, signed. “I’m not saying it’s impossible, but there would have to be safeguards,” Rogers admits, and briefly shows signs of provisionalism, the willingness to compromise for the sake of the relationship. However, he immediately takes back his gesture when he learns Tony has already detained Wanda, a fellow Avenger, because she, like Rogers, didn’t sign the accords. He immediately gets mad, as any chance of him signing the accords is thrown out the window. “Give me a break! I’m doing what has to be done to stave off something worse,” Tony yells. “You keep telling yourself that,” Rogers says before leaving the room. Here, Rogers demonstrates the exit response once more, which proves to be harmful to their relationship this time around. It is important to note, however, that although Rogers and Stark try and respond with voice, and actively seek solutions, Rogers always exits. In this scenario, it may seem like the correct thing to do, as both are set in their ways and avoiding the conflict will spare them time and energy. However, (Wood, 2014) states, “Because exit doesn’t address problems, it tends to be destructive” (p. 260). This statement holds true, as the next time Rogers and Stark see each other at an empty airport, they end up physically fighting, which further destroys their relationship.

However, right before their showdown at the airport, Rogers and Stark talk briefly. During those few moments of dialogue they could have solved the issue peaceably, but don’t because, once again, they use disconfirming communication tactics. “You are after the wrong guy,” says Rogers. Not only is Rogers demonstrating certainty again, but also evaluation, which is another form of disconfirming communication. Evaluation is a way of judging the other person (Wood, 2014). Notice Rogers doesn’t try and persuade Stark that he is after the wrong guy, but tells him that he is. There are a lot of implications in evaluations that cause the receiver of them to become defensive. Stark uses similar disconfirming communication. “Your judgement is askew,” he tells Rogers, implying that Rogers is crazy. Notice how they aren’t even discussing the problem anymore. They are just calling each other out. According to (Rusbult, Zembrodt & Gunn, 1982), “refusing to discuss problems, criticizing the [other] for things unrelated to the real problem,” and “treating the [other] badly emotionally or physically,” are signs of a neglectful response to conflict (p. 1231). I argue that, in Civil War, fighting can be categorized similarly. They’re done with talking and they couldn’t care about the status of their relationship at this point. They are just trying to prove their point. This response makes sense, considering (Beatty & Dobos, 1993) suggest, “Disconfirming treatment inhibits one's sense of ‘interconnectedness’ with others, and encourages interpersonal disconnectedness. This detachment leads to insensitivity, a condition underlying the undesirable hyper masculine behaviors” (p. 188). It is no surprise then, that two highly masculine friends fight to resolve a conflict when their disconfirming communication methods aren’t enough to solve the problem.

Ultimately, the final fight between Stark and Rogers could have been avoided altogether. So, I offer my advice to both Stark and Rogers, though I aim it mostly at Rogers because, if he had self-disclosed valuable information, he could have prevented the final standoff with Stark from ever happening. By sharing, early on, what knowledge he had, and by expressing his motivation for doing what he was doing, he could’ve saved a lot of time and energy. Eventually, Rogers realizes this himself, and he writes to Tony at the end of the movie, saying,

“I’m sorry. Hopefully one day you can understand. I wish we agreed on the Accords, I really do. I know you were only doing what you believe in, and that’s all any of us can do, it’s all any of us should. So, no matter what, I promise if you — if you need us. If you need me, I’ll be there.”

This is an act of self disclosure, which means the relationship might rekindle and trust might be regained. However, they may have already taken this too far. The damage may be unrepairable. This is my advice: Firstly, self-disclose before the conflict gets to be too much, instead of as a way to try and repair the damage. Secondly, self-disclose often with those you care about. It will build trust and deepen your understanding of one another.

To recap, In Civil War, Rogers and Stark repeatedly engage in disconfirming communication, which isn’t a good method to proactively find solutions. They were certain they were right, when they could have used provisionalism to listen and be open to each other’s ideas and experiences. Also, Rogers relied too often on exiting to respond to the conflict, which always led to a more intense follow-up discussion or fight later. When Stark chose to self-disclose, they almost came to an agreement, but didn’t because neither could see past their own point of view. Later, Rogers also uses self-disclosure in an attempt to rekindle their relationship, but by that time the damage had been done. Like Fox News, KCNA also has a variety of tactics they use to portray President Trump in a certain way. KCNA uses strong language to describe Trump, which often manifests itself in the form of insults. KCNA insults President Trump in 9 of the 25 articles. These insults read as facts because they are not presented as subjective quotes from individual sources. For example, KCNA uses insults such as lunatic, war maniac, and old man to describe Trump. North Korea’s insistent on making more weapons, and doesn’t show signs of stopping. They have a cause, according to KCNA, that they are willing to sacrifice everything for. KCNA, out of the 25 articles I analyzed, never presented, or even commented on, views that oppose those of Kim Jong Un.
